# Water Purification Methods

Access to clean drinking water is critical for survival. Contaminated water can cause dysentery, cholera, giardiasis, cryptosporidiosis, and other waterborne illnesses. This document covers practical purification methods ranked by reliability and ease of use.

## Boiling

Boiling is the most reliable field purification method. Bring water to a rolling boil and maintain for at least 1 minute. At altitudes above 2000 meters (6500 feet), boil for 3 minutes because water boils at a lower temperature at altitude. Boiling kills bacteria, viruses, and parasites including Giardia and Cryptosporidium. It does not remove chemical contaminants, heavy metals, or sediment. Allow water to cool naturally in a clean container. Boiled water may taste flat — pouring it back and forth between two clean containers adds air and improves taste.

## Chemical Treatment with Chlorine

Unscented liquid household bleach containing 5 to 9 percent sodium hypochlorite can purify water. Add 2 drops per liter of clear water (4 drops if water is cloudy or cold). Stir and let stand for 30 minutes. The water should have a slight chlorine smell after treatment — if not, repeat the dose and wait another 15 minutes. Chlorine is effective against most bacteria and viruses but less reliable against Cryptosporidium. Commercial water purification tablets (sodium dichloroisocyanurate or NaDCC) follow the same principle with pre-measured doses.

## Chemical Treatment with Iodine

Iodine-based purification uses either tincture of iodine (2 percent) or iodine tablets. For tincture, add 5 drops per liter of clear water (10 drops if cloudy). Wait 30 minutes before drinking. Iodine is effective against bacteria, viruses, and Giardia but less effective against Cryptosporidium. Iodine-treated water has a distinctive taste that can be reduced by adding vitamin C (ascorbic acid) after the treatment wait time. Do not use iodine purification for more than a few weeks continuously. Iodine is not recommended for pregnant women or people with thyroid conditions.

## Filtration

Portable water filters physically remove pathogens by forcing water through a filter medium. Filters rated to 0.2 microns or smaller remove bacteria and protozoa. Most portable filters do NOT remove viruses — a chemical treatment step or a purifier-rated filter (0.02 microns) is needed for virus protection. Ceramic filters can be cleaned and reused many times. Hollow fiber filters are lightweight and pack small. Always follow manufacturer instructions for flow direction and backflushing.

Improvised filtration using layers of sand, gravel, and charcoal can reduce sediment and some contaminants but should not be relied upon as the sole purification method. Always follow improvised filtration with boiling or chemical treatment.

## UV Treatment

Ultraviolet light disrupts the DNA of pathogens, rendering them unable to reproduce. Commercial UV purifiers (such as SteriPEN) treat 1 liter in 60 to 90 seconds. UV treatment is effective against bacteria, viruses, and protozoa including Cryptosporidium. Limitations: water must be clear (turbidity blocks UV penetration), the device requires batteries or charging, and there is no residual protection (treated water can be recontaminated).

Solar disinfection (SODIS) uses sunlight as a UV source. Fill a clear plastic or glass bottle with clear water and place in direct sunlight for at least 6 hours (or 2 days if cloudy). UV-A radiation and heat work together to kill pathogens. SODIS is free and requires no equipment beyond a clear bottle but is slow and weather-dependent.

## Distillation

Distillation involves boiling water and collecting the steam, which condenses back into liquid water. This removes bacteria, viruses, parasites, heavy metals, salts, and most chemical contaminants. It is the most thorough purification method but requires significant fuel and equipment. A field-improvised solar still can produce small amounts of distilled water by evaporating moisture from soil or vegetation under a plastic sheet, with a collection container below the low point of the sheet.

## Water Source Selection

When choosing a water source in the field, prefer flowing water over standing water. Flowing water has lower parasite and bacteria concentrations. Avoid water downstream of human habitation, agriculture, or industry. Springs emerging from the ground are generally safer than surface water. Clear water is not necessarily safe — many pathogens are invisible. Always purify regardless of apparent clarity.

## Storage After Purification

Purified water can be recontaminated by dirty containers, hands, or airborne debris. Store purified water in clean, covered containers. Do not dip hands or dirty utensils into the container. If using chemical treatment, the residual chlorine or iodine provides some ongoing protection. Boiled or UV-treated water has no residual protection and should be consumed relatively quickly or stored very carefully.
