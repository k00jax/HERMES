# First Aid Fundamentals

This document provides concise, actionable first aid guidance for field situations where professional medical care may be delayed or unavailable.

## Bleeding Control

For minor cuts and abrasions, clean the wound with clean water, apply direct pressure with a clean cloth, and cover with a bandage once bleeding stops. For deep or heavily bleeding wounds, apply firm direct pressure and do not remove the pressure cloth — add more layers on top if blood soaks through. Elevate the injured limb above heart level if possible. For life-threatening extremity bleeding, apply a tourniquet 2 to 3 inches above the wound (never on a joint). Note the time the tourniquet was applied. A tourniquet should only be loosened by medical professionals.

## Burns

For minor burns (redness, no blisters), cool the burn under cool running water for at least 10 minutes. Do not use ice. Do not apply butter, oil, or toothpaste. Cover loosely with a clean non-stick dressing. For burns with blisters, do not pop the blisters. Cool with water, cover loosely, and seek medical attention. For severe burns (charred or white skin, deep tissue damage), do not remove clothing stuck to the burn. Cover with a clean dry cloth. Treat for shock and seek emergency care immediately.

## Fractures and Sprains

If a broken bone is suspected, immobilize the limb in the position found. Do not attempt to realign the bone. Splint the injury using rigid material (sticks, rolled magazines, trekking poles) padded with cloth, securing above and below the fracture site. Check circulation below the splint periodically — fingers or toes should remain warm and pink. For sprains, follow RICE: Rest, Ice (or cold water), Compression with an elastic bandage, and Elevation.

## Heat Exhaustion

Symptoms include heavy sweating, weakness, cold and clammy skin, nausea, dizziness, and headache. Move the person to shade or a cool area. Remove excess clothing. Apply cool wet cloths to the neck, armpits, and forehead. Give small sips of water if the person is conscious and not vomiting. Rest for at least 30 minutes before any further activity. If symptoms worsen or the person stops sweating, suspect heatstroke.

## Heatstroke

Heatstroke is a medical emergency. Symptoms include hot dry skin (sweating may have stopped), core temperature above 40 C, confusion, slurred speech, seizures, or loss of consciousness. Cool the person aggressively: immerse in cool water if possible, or apply wet cloths and fan vigorously. Focus cooling on the neck, armpits, and groin where major blood vessels are close to the skin. Do not give fluids if the person is unconscious. Seek emergency medical care immediately.

## Hypothermia

Symptoms progress through stages: shivering and numbness (mild), violent shivering and confusion (moderate), shivering stops and drowsiness sets in (severe). For mild hypothermia, move to shelter, remove wet clothing, and warm gradually with dry layers, warm drinks, and body heat. For moderate to severe hypothermia, handle the person gently — rough movement can trigger cardiac arrest. Wrap in blankets or a sleeping bag. Apply heat packs to the chest, neck, and groin (not extremities first). Do not give alcohol. Seek emergency medical care.

## Dehydration

Signs include dark urine, dry mouth, headache, dizziness, and fatigue. Mild dehydration can be corrected by drinking water in small frequent sips. Adding a pinch of salt and sugar to water creates an improvised oral rehydration solution (approximately half a teaspoon of salt and 6 teaspoons of sugar per liter of clean water). Severe dehydration (confusion, rapid heartbeat, inability to keep fluids down) requires medical attention.

## Choking

If the person can cough forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing. Do not slap their back while they are upright and coughing effectively. If the person cannot breathe, speak, or cough, perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver): stand behind the person, place your fist just above the navel, grasp with your other hand, and thrust inward and upward. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person loses consciousness.

## Allergic Reactions

Mild allergic reactions (localized swelling, hives, itching) can be treated with antihistamines if available. For severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) — symptoms include throat swelling, difficulty breathing, rapid pulse, and dizziness — use an epinephrine auto-injector if available, injecting into the outer thigh. Call for emergency help. Have the person lie down with legs elevated unless they are having difficulty breathing, in which case they should sit upright. A second dose of epinephrine may be needed if symptoms do not improve in 5 to 15 minutes.

## Bites and Stings

For insect stings, remove the stinger by scraping sideways (do not squeeze with tweezers). Clean the area and apply a cold compress. Monitor for allergic reaction. For snake bites, keep the person calm and still. Immobilize the bitten limb and keep it below heart level. Do not cut the wound, suck out venom, or apply a tourniquet. Note the snake's appearance if safely possible. Seek medical care. For tick bites, remove the tick by grasping close to the skin with fine tweezers and pulling straight out with steady pressure. Clean the area. Save the tick for identification if possible. Monitor for rash or fever over the following weeks.

## Wound Infection Signs

Signs that a wound may be infected include increasing redness spreading from the wound, warmth around the wound, swelling, increasing pain, pus or cloudy drainage, red streaks extending from the wound toward the heart, fever, and swollen lymph nodes near the wound. Infected wounds require medical attention. In the field, keep the wound clean, change dressings regularly, and monitor closely.
