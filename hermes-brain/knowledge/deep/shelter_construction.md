# Shelter Construction Principles

Shelter is a survival priority second only to immediate medical needs. Exposure to wind, rain, cold, or extreme heat can lead to hypothermia, heatstroke, or dehydration faster than lack of food or water. This document covers principles and techniques for constructing field shelters.

## Site Selection

Choose a site that is protected from prevailing wind. Avoid hilltops and ridgelines where wind exposure is maximum. Avoid valley floors and depressions where cold air pools at night and flooding is possible. Avoid dry riverbeds and drainage channels — flash floods can occur without local rainfall. Look for natural windbreaks like rock faces, fallen trees, or dense vegetation. Ensure the site is not under dead branches or unstable rock. Flat or slightly sloped ground is ideal — a slight slope aids drainage.

## Lean-To Shelter

The lean-to is the simplest and fastest shelter to construct. Find or place a horizontal ridgepole between two supports (trees, forked sticks, or rock features) at approximately waist height. Lean branches, poles, or large pieces of bark against the ridgepole at a 45 to 60 degree angle. Layer smaller branches, leaves, pine needles, or grass thickly over the frame to create a waterproof and wind-resistant covering. The open side should face away from the prevailing wind. A lean-to provides overhead and wind protection but limited insulation. Build a fire reflector (stacked logs or a rock wall) on the open side to radiate heat into the shelter.

## A-Frame Shelter

An A-frame provides better protection than a lean-to in heavy rain or cold. Secure a ridgepole between two supports. Lean branches on both sides to create a triangular cross-section. Make the interior just large enough to lie in — smaller volume retains body heat better. Layer covering material thickly, starting from the bottom and overlapping upward like shingles so rain runs off. Close one end and leave the other open or partially covered for entry.

## Debris Hut

A debris hut is an insulated A-frame designed for cold weather survival. Build a low A-frame structure just tall enough to crawl into. Pile leaves, pine needles, grass, and other dry debris over the entire structure to a thickness of at least 60 centimeters (2 feet). The thicker the debris layer, the better the insulation. Fill the interior floor with a thick layer of dry debris as bedding — ground contact is a major source of heat loss. A properly built debris hut can maintain survivable temperatures even in freezing conditions using body heat alone.

## Tarp and Poncho Shelters

If you have a tarp, poncho, or emergency space blanket, shelter construction becomes much faster and more waterproof. A tarp can be rigged as a lean-to, A-frame, or flat roof using cordage and anchor points. Key configurations include the ridge tent (tarp draped over a ridgeline cord), the lean-to (one edge tied high, opposite staked low), and the body bag (tarp wrapped around you with feet toward wind). Always secure edges to prevent wind from lifting the tarp. Guy lines and stakes improve wind resistance significantly.

## Snow Shelters

In snow conditions, snow itself is an excellent insulator. A snow trench is the simplest — dig a trench in deep snow, lie in it, and cover the top with branches or a tarp. A quinzhee is built by piling loose snow into a mound at least 1.5 meters high, letting it sinter (settle and bond) for 2 hours, then hollowing out the interior. Wall thickness should be at least 30 centimeters. Poke ventilation holes with a stick. The entrance should be lower than the sleeping platform so cold air sinks away from the occupant. Interior temperature in a snow shelter typically stabilizes near 0 C regardless of external temperature.

## Ground Insulation

Sleeping directly on the ground causes rapid heat loss through conduction. Always create an insulation layer between your body and the ground. Use dry leaves, pine needles, grass, moss, bark strips, or a foam pad. The insulation layer should be at least 10 to 15 centimeters thick when compressed. In cold conditions, ground insulation is more important than overhead cover for surviving the night.

## Ventilation

All shelters need some airflow to prevent condensation buildup and CO2 accumulation. A sealed shelter with a fire or stove inside can become dangerously low in oxygen. Even body heat and breathing in a sealed debris hut or snow shelter will raise humidity and CO2 — leave a small opening for air exchange. HERMES CO2 and humidity monitoring is directly relevant here: rising CO2 inside a shelter indicates insufficient ventilation.

## Heat Retention Principles

Smaller shelters retain heat better than large ones. Build only as large as needed. Block wind entry points. Use reflective surfaces (emergency blankets, aluminum foil, light-colored rock) to bounce radiated heat back toward you. A fire reflector wall (stacked green logs or flat rocks) between a fire and your shelter opening dramatically increases warmth. Dark-colored materials absorb and radiate heat; use them on the fire-facing side.

## Shelter in Urban and Vehicle Environments

In urban survival situations, vehicles provide immediate shelter from wind and rain. Crack windows slightly for ventilation. In abandoned buildings, choose interior rooms away from broken windows. Check structural integrity before entering. In all cases, HERMES temperature and humidity monitoring helps assess whether the shelter environment is maintaining safe conditions.
