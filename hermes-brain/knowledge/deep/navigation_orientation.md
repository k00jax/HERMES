# Navigation and Orientation Without GPS

When electronic navigation fails or is unavailable, basic orientation skills can prevent disorientation and enable purposeful movement. This document covers compass-free navigation techniques suitable for field use.

## Sun-Based Direction Finding

In the Northern Hemisphere, the sun rises in the east, reaches its highest point due south, and sets in the west. At solar noon (when the sun is highest), shadows point due north. The sun moves approximately 15 degrees per hour across the sky, so time can be used to estimate direction if you know the approximate hour.

In the Southern Hemisphere, the sun reaches its highest point due north at solar noon, and shadows point due south.

## Shadow Stick Method

Place a straight stick vertically in the ground on a sunny day. Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone. Wait 15 to 30 minutes and mark the new shadow tip position. A line drawn from the first mark to the second runs approximately east-west (first mark is west, second is east) because shadows move from west to east as the sun moves from east to west. A perpendicular line gives the north-south axis.

## Watch Compass Method

With an analog watch in the Northern Hemisphere: point the hour hand at the sun. The midpoint between the hour hand and the 12 o'clock position indicates south. In the Southern Hemisphere, point the 12 o'clock position at the sun. The midpoint between 12 and the hour hand indicates north. For digital watches, visualize where the hands would be. This method is approximate and works best at mid-latitudes away from the tropics.

## Star Navigation: Northern Hemisphere

The North Star (Polaris) marks true north within about 1 degree. To find it, locate the Big Dipper (Ursa Major). The two stars forming the outer edge of the bowl (the pointer stars) point directly to Polaris. Polaris is the last star in the handle of the Little Dipper (Ursa Minor). Polaris appears stationary while all other stars rotate around it during the night. Its altitude above the horizon equals your latitude.

## Star Navigation: Southern Hemisphere

The Southern Hemisphere has no single pole star. Use the Southern Cross (Crux) to find south. Extend the long axis of the Southern Cross 4.5 times its length past the bottom star. That point in the sky is approximately above the South Celestial Pole. Drop a line from that point straight down to the horizon to find due south.

## Moon Direction Estimation

When the moon is a crescent, draw an imaginary line through the two tips of the crescent down to the horizon. In the Northern Hemisphere, this point is approximately south. A full moon rises in the east around sunset and sets in the west around sunrise, following the same general path as the sun.

## Natural Indicators

Natural direction indicators are unreliable individually but useful in combination. Moss tends to grow on the shadier side of trees and rocks (north side in Northern Hemisphere, south side in Southern Hemisphere) but this varies greatly with local conditions. Spider webs are more common on the warmer, sunnier side. Prevailing wind direction shapes vegetation — trees may lean or have denser growth on the sheltered side. Snow melts faster on sun-facing slopes. These indicators should only supplement more reliable methods.

## Terrain Association

If you have a map (paper), orient it to the landscape by aligning visible features: ridgelines, rivers, road intersections, distinctive peaks. Even without a compass, a properly oriented map lets you determine direction from known features. Follow linear features (rivers, ridgelines, roads, power lines) to maintain consistent direction of travel. They also serve as handrails to prevent lateral drift.

## Dead Reckoning

Dead reckoning means tracking your position by estimating distance and direction from a known starting point. Count paces to estimate distance (most people take approximately 60 to 70 double paces per 100 meters on flat ground, more on hills). Pick a distant landmark in your direction of travel and walk to it, then pick the next one. This prevents gradual drift off course.

## Staying Found

The most important navigation principle is maintaining awareness of your position at all times. Before leaving a known location, note distinctive features around you and your direction of travel. Look behind you periodically — the return route looks different from the outbound route. Mark your trail if necessary (stacked rocks, broken branches, flagging tape). If you become disoriented, stop moving. Sit down, calm down, and think. Continuing to move while disoriented usually makes the situation worse.

## HERMES Relevance

HERMES does not currently have a magnetometer or GPS module. Future hardware revisions could add a magnetometer for digital compass functionality. In the current configuration, HERMES can still assist navigation by logging timestamps and providing ambient condition context (temperature dropping suggests approaching nightfall or higher elevation, humidity rising suggests approaching water).
